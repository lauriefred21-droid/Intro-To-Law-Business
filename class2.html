The Three Types of Transaction Costs

1. Search Costs

Definition: The cost of finding the right party to contract with.

Examples:

  • Time spent researching potential suppliers or contractors
  • Reading reviews and comparing options
  • Vetting candidates for a job
  • Traveling to meet potential business partners

How lawyers reduce search costs:

  • Maintain networks of trusted counterparties and vendors
  • Know market rates and industry standards
  • Can quickly vet potential partners using reputation and track record
  • Provide referrals based on past experience

2. Bargaining Costs

Definition: The cost of negotiating terms and reaching an agreement.

Examples:

  • Time spent in negotiation meetings
  • Back-and-forth haggling over price and contract terms
  • Drafting and reviewing multiple versions of proposals
  • Dealing with contingencies and edge cases

How lawyers reduce bargaining costs:

  • Use standard form contracts (no need to reinvent the wheel)
  • Know market norms for deal terms
  • Focus negotiation on key issues rather than every detail
  • Clear communication reduces rounds of back-and-forth

3. Enforcement Costs

Definition: The cost of ensuring the other party performs as agreed.

Examples:

  • Monitoring quality and compliance
  • Conducting audits and inspections
  • Resolving disputes when things go wrong
  • Litigation and court costs if breach occurs

How lawyers reduce enforcement costs:

  • Draft clear, unambiguous contract language
  • Include dispute resolution clauses (arbitration, mediation)
  • Set up escrow arrangements or performance bonds
  • Build in remedies and liquidated damages provisions

Case Study: Boeing & Spirit AeroSystems

🛫 What Happened

Original Setup: Boeing manufactured fuselages (the main body of aircraft) in-house at their own facilities with their own employees.

The Decision: In the early 2000s, Boeing decided to outsource fuselage production to Spirit AeroSystems to reduce costs and allow Boeing to focus on core competencies like design and final assembly.

The Problems That Emerged:

  • Significant quality defects in delivered fuselages
  • Chronic delivery delays disrupting Boeing's production schedule
  • Misaligned incentives—Spirit optimized for its own profit margins, not Boeing's quality standards
  • Difficulty coordinating complex production changes
  • Spirit made design choices that benefited Spirit but created problems for Boeing's integration process

The Result: Boeing eventually had to bring some production back in-house and massively increase oversight of Spirit, essentially paying both the transaction costs AND the monitoring costs. Damage to quality, reputation, and the 737 MAX program was substantial.

The Make vs Buy Framework

Option 1: OUTSOURCE (Buy from Market)

Advantages:

  • Lower transaction costs—supplier handles hiring, management, facilities
  • Specialization benefits—Spirit focuses only on fuselages
  • No capital investment required from Boeing
  • Flexibility to switch suppliers if needed

Disadvantages:

  • Higher monitoring costs—need extensive quality control
  • Less control—can't directly manage Spirit's workers or processes
  • Misaligned incentives—Spirit wants profit, Boeing wants quality
  • Coordination problems—changes require negotiation, not management directive

Option 2: MAKE IN-HOUSE (Hierarchy)

Advantages:

  • Lower monitoring costs—direct control over production
  • Better quality control—can immediately address issues
  • Aligned incentives—everyone works toward same company goals
  • Easier coordination—make changes through management, not contracts

Disadvantages:

  • Higher transaction costs—must handle everything (hiring, managing, facilities)
  • Capital intensive—need to invest in equipment and infrastructure
  • Less flexibility—harder to exit if conditions change
  • Dilutes focus from core competencies

🔥 The Critical Tradeoff

Firms draw their boundaries where:

Transaction Costs of Market = Monitoring Costs of Hierarchy

When monitoring costs outweigh transaction costs → Make in-house

When transaction costs outweigh monitoring costs → Buy from market

Why Boeing Should Have Kept Fuselages In-House

Fuselage production has these characteristics:

  • High specificity: Custom designed for each Boeing aircraft model
  • Critical quality requirements: Safety-critical component—lives depend on it
  • Complex integration: Requires tight coordination with wings, tail, systems
  • Core to product: Not a commodity component that can be easily swapped

Result: When something has these traits, monitoring costs are extremely high. Better to make in-house.

General Rule for Make vs Buy:

  • The more specific/complex/critical the component...
  • The higher the monitoring costs if outsourced...
  • The more likely you should make it in-house

Counterexample: Coca-Cola's Bottling Strategy

Coca-Cola does the opposite—they outsource bottling to local franchisees worldwide.

Why outsourcing works for Coke:

  • Low specificity: Bottling is a standardized, repeatable process
  • Low integration needs: Bottlers don't need constant coordination with Coke HQ
  • Easy to monitor: Simple quality checks and brand standards
  • Local knowledge valuable: Bottlers understand their regional markets better than HQ would

Result: Transaction costs of managing local bottling < Monitoring costs of franchise system → Outsource it!

Coase Theorem: Why Firms Exist

The Fundamental Question

Why do firms exist at all? Why not just have everyone operate as independent contractors and use markets for everything?

Ronald Coase's Answer (1937 Nobel Prize-winning insight):

Firms exist to minimize transaction costs.

In a world with zero transaction costs, we wouldn't need firms—everyone would just contract with everyone else for every task. But because markets have friction (search, bargaining, enforcement costs), it's often cheaper to bring activities inside a firm.

The Thought Experiment

Imagine: Perfect Markets (Zero Transaction Costs)

  • Finding the right person for any task: free and instant
  • Negotiating deal terms: free and instant
  • Ensuring they do the work: free and automatic
  • → You'd just contract separately for every single task
  • → No need for firms or employees!

Reality: High Transaction Costs

  • Finding the right person: expensive, time-consuming
  • Negotiating every task: exhausting, slow
  • Ensuring quality work: requires monitoring, enforcement
  • → Better to hire employees under long-term contracts
  • → Use management hierarchy instead of markets

How Firms Reduce Transaction Costs

Instead of using markets for every task, firms:

1. Hire employees under long-term employment contracts

  • One search process, one negotiation → covers hundreds of tasks
  • Don't need to renegotiate for each new assignment
  • Employment relationship implies broad duties within job scope

2. Use hierarchy and management authority

  • Managers can direct employees to tasks without new contracts
  • No bargaining needed for routine work assignments
  • Authority relation replaces market transactions

3. Internalize coordination

  • Employees work together under shared systems and culture
  • Common processes reduce need to specify everything
  • Informal coordination is easier within firm boundaries

The Boundary of the Firm

When does a firm stop expanding?

A firm expands its boundaries until: Cost of internal coordination = Cost of market transaction

Example: Startup Growth Trajectory

Stage 1: Solo Founder

  • Does everything personally (product, sales, admin)
  • Very low coordination costs (it's just you!)

Stage 2: First Hires

  • Hire core team for critical functions
  • Still cheaper to hire than to outsource every task
  • Coordination costs still manageable

Stage 3: Selective Outsourcing

  • Start contracting out non-core functions
  • Janitorial services: easier to hire contractor than manage janitors
  • IT support: specialists can do it more efficiently
  • For these tasks: Market cost < Internal coordination cost

Stage 4: Mature Company

  • Clear boundaries between core (in-house) and non-core (outsourced)
  • Continuously evaluate make vs buy for each function

Show Sample Answer

Transaction costs are the costs of using the market to conduct economic exchanges.

1. Search Costs: The cost of finding the right party to contract with.

  • Example: Spending hours researching plumbers online, reading reviews, comparing prices, and calling multiple companies before hiring one to fix a leak.

2. Bargaining Costs: The cost of negotiating terms and reaching agreement.

3. Enforcement Costs: The cost of ensuring the other party performs as agreed.

  • Example: Inspecting a contractor's work to ensure it meets code, withholding final payment until issues are fixed, or taking them to small claims court if they don't complete the job.

Question 3: Boeing/Spirit Analysis

Boeing originally outsourced fuselage production to Spirit AeroSystems but encountered significant problems. Using the framework of transaction costs versus monitoring costs, explain why a firm might choose to produce a component in-house rather than contracting with an outside supplier. What does the Boeing/Spirit relationship illustrate about the tradeoff firms face when deciding where to draw the boundaries of the firm?

Show Sample Answer

The Framework:

  • Outsourcing lowers transaction costs (no need to hire, train, manage workers; no capital investment) but increases monitoring costs (need to ensure supplier delivers quality on time)
  • In-house production raises transaction costs (must handle all aspects of production) but lowers monitoring costs (direct control over process and quality)

Why In-House Can Be Better:

For components that are:

  • Highly specific to the firm's needs
  • Safety-critical or quality-sensitive
  • Require complex integration with other parts
  • Core to the product

...the monitoring costs of outsourcing become prohibitively high, making in-house production the better choice.

Boeing/Spirit Illustration:

  • Fuselages are highly specific to each aircraft model, safety-critical, and require tight integration with wings, tail, and systems
  • When Boeing outsourced to Spirit, they encountered quality defects, delivery delays, and misaligned incentives
  • Spirit optimized for its own profit, not Boeing's quality standards
  • The monitoring costs (extensive oversight, quality control, dealing with defects) exceeded the transaction costs Boeing would have faced by keeping production in-house

The Tradeoff:

Firms draw their boundaries where the cost of using the market (including monitoring external suppliers) equals the cost of internal organization. Boeing misjudged this tradeoff for fuselages—a component too critical and complex to outsource effectively.

Question 4: Coase Theorem Application

According to Coase, why do firms exist? In a world with zero transaction costs, would we need firms at all? Explain your reasoning.

Show Sample Answer

Why Firms Exist:

According to Coase, firms exist to minimize transaction costs. Using markets for every task involves costs of:

  • Finding the right people (search costs)
  • Negotiating each transaction (bargaining costs)
  • Ensuring performance (enforcement costs)

Firms reduce these costs by bringing activities inside organizational boundaries, using employment relationships and management hierarchy instead of repeated market transactions.

Zero Transaction Costs World:

If transaction costs were zero, we wouldn't need firms. Everyone could operate as independent contractors because:

  • Finding the perfect person for any task would be instant and free
  • Negotiating terms would be effortless
  • Ensuring performance would be automatic and costless

In this hypothetical world, markets would work perfectly for coordinating all economic activity, and the firm as an organizational form would serve no purpose.

But because transaction costs are real and significant, firms emerge as a superior way to organize certain activities—specifically those where the transaction costs of using markets exceed the coordination costs of internal management.
