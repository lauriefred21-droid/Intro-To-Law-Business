Relational Contracts vs Ordinary Contracts

Ordinary Contracts (One-Shot)

Characteristics:

  • Single transaction
  • Clear price and deliverables
  • Happens once and done
  • Easy to specify everything upfront
  • If breach, walk away and sue

Examples:

  • Buying a car
  • One-time consulting project
  • Construction contract for a house

Relational Contracts (Long-Term)

Characteristics:

  • Ongoing relationship over years
  • Future uncertain (can't specify everything)
  • Requires flexibility and adaptation
  • Both parties invest in relationship
  • If problems arise, work it out (relationship has value)

Examples:

  • Supplier relationships (like BSC/Medinol)
  • Employment agreements
  • Franchise agreements
  • Joint ventures

Key Differences

Aspect Ordinary Relational
Duration One-time Long-term
Specificity Detailed terms Flexible frameworks
Dispute Response Sue and leave Negotiate and adapt
Trust Don't need much Essential
Adaptation Unnecessary Expected

The BSC/Medinol Case Study

Background

The Parties:

  • Boston Scientific (BSC): Large medical device company
  • Medinol: Small Israeli company that invented a new stent technology

The Deal:

  • Medinol develops and manufactures stents
  • BSC markets and sells them worldwide
  • Long-term exclusive relationship
  • Both parties make huge investments in the relationship

Why This Is a Relational Contract

  1. Long-term relationship (multi-year commitment)
  2. Mutual dependence (neither can succeed without the other)
  3. Relationship-specific investments:
    • Medinol invests in production capacity for BSC
    • BSC invests in training sales force on Medinol's stent
    • Both invest in regulatory approvals
  4. Uncertain future (can't predict all market changes, tech developments)
  5. Requires ongoing cooperation (product improvements, quality issues, volume forecasts)

The Contract Provisions

1. Forecasting Requirements

What it says: BSC provides volume forecasts to Medinol

Why it matters: Medinol needs to know how much capacity to build

The problem: Forecasts are just estimates, not binding commitments

2. Alternative Product Lines

What it says: BSC can develop and sell competing stent products

Why BSC wants this: Don't want to be locked in if Medinol fails or better tech comes along

Medinol's concern: BSC might push their own product instead of Medinol's

3. Integration Clause

What it says: "This written contract is the entire agreement; no side promises count"

Why it matters: Prevents parties from claiming there were other verbal agreements

In court: Judge can only look at written contract, not "he told me" claims

What Went Wrong

The Conflict:

  1. BSC gave Medinol forecasts showing huge growth
  2. Medinol invested millions to build production capacity
  3. BSC's actual orders were MUCH lower than forecasts
  4. Medinol left with excess capacity and huge costs
  5. Medinol claimed BSC acted in bad faith

BSC's Defense:

  • Forecasts are just estimates, not commitments
  • Contract explicitly says BSC has no obligation to buy forecasted amounts
  • Market conditions changed unexpectedly
  • BSC was also selling competing products (which contract allowed)

Medinol's Argument:

  • BSC gave unrealistic forecasts knowing Medinol would rely on them
  • BSC pushed their own competing products instead of Medinol's
  • This violated the spirit of the partnership even if not the letter
  • BSC acted in bad faith

The Legal Issues

1. Are Forecasts Binding?

Generally NO - forecasts are predictions, not promises

BUT: If given in bad faith (deliberately misleading), might be actionable

2. Duty of Good Faith

All contracts have implied duty of good faith and fair dealing

Question: Did BSC violate this by:

  • Giving inflated forecasts?
  • Pushing competing products?
  • Not trying to meet forecasts?

3. Relational Contract Flexibility vs Certainty

The tension:

  • Relational contracts need flexibility (can't predict future)
  • BUT parties need some certainty to make investments
  • How much can BSC deviate from forecasts before it's bad faith?

Key Contract Provisions in Commercial Deals

1. Price and Payment Terms

  • Fixed price vs cost-plus
  • Volume discounts
  • Payment schedule (Net 30, Net 60, etc.)
  • Price adjustment clauses (inflation, materials costs)

2. Delivery and Performance

  • Delivery schedules and deadlines
  • Quality standards and specifications
  • Acceptance testing procedures
  • Remedies for late delivery or defects

3. Warranties and Representations

  • Product quality guarantees
  • Fitness for particular purpose
  • No infringement of IP
  • Disclaimer of implied warranties

4. Intellectual Property

  • Who owns developed IP
  • License grants
  • Confidentiality obligations
  • Use restrictions

5. Termination

  • Termination for cause (breach)
  • Termination for convenience
  • Notice requirements
  • Wind-down obligations
  • Survival of certain provisions

6. Dispute Resolution

  • Escalation procedures (talk to manager first, then VP, then arbitration)
  • Arbitration vs litigation
  • Choice of law
  • Venue/jurisdiction

7. Force Majeure

What it is: Excuses performance for events beyond control

Typical events: Natural disasters, wars, pandemics, government actions

Effect: Suspends obligations without penalty during force majeure event

💡 Exam Tips for Contracts

  • Know the difference: Relational = long-term + flexibility, Ordinary = one-shot + detailed
  • BSC/Medinol shows tension between flexibility and certainty in relational contracts
  • Forecasts are generally not binding commitments
  • Good faith duty applies to all contracts (can't deliberately undermine deal)
  • Integration clause = only written contract counts, not side promises
