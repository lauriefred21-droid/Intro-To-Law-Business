Class 3: Entity Choice

Choosing the right business structure and understanding tax implications

🎧

Class 3 Podcast

Listen first for overview (~12 minutes)

🎯 EXAM FOCUS: The tax comparison calculation (C-Corp vs pass-through on $100k profit) WILL appear with different numbers. Know why VCs require C-Corps despite higher taxes. S-Corp details and complex tax scenarios are NOT tested.

The Four Main Entity Types

1. C-Corporation

Taxation: Double taxation (corporate level + personal level)

Key Features:

  • Can have unlimited shareholders
  • Can have different classes of stock (preferred + common)
  • Foreign investors allowed
  • Most formal structure with board requirements

Best For: High-growth startups seeking venture capital funding, companies planning to go public

2. S-Corporation

Taxation: Pass-through (only personal tax, no corporate tax)

Key Features:

  • Maximum 100 shareholders
  • Only US citizens/residents can be shareholders
  • Only ONE class of stock allowed
  • Corporate formalities required (board meetings, minutes)

Best For: Small to medium businesses with modest growth, US-only investors, want tax benefits but need corporate structure

3. LLC (Limited Liability Company)

Taxation: Pass-through by default (can elect to be taxed as C-Corp)

Key Features:

  • No limit on number of members
  • Foreign investors allowed
  • Very flexible operating agreement
  • Less formal than corporations
  • Can customize member rights and distributions

Best For: Flexible structure needed, real estate investments, partnerships, small businesses prioritizing simplicity

4. Partnership / Sole Proprietorship

Taxation: Pass-through

Key Features:

  • No liability protection in general partnership
  • Simplest to set up
  • Partners personally liable for business debts
  • Minimal formalities

Best For: Very small businesses, service providers who don't need liability protection, sole operators

The Critical Tax Calculation

🔥 This WILL Be On Your Exam

You need to be able to calculate and compare tax burdens under C-Corp vs pass-through taxation with different profit amounts.

The Scenario

A business earns $100,000 in profit before taxes.

Compare the total tax burden and owner take-home under:

  1. C-Corporation (double taxation)
  2. Pass-through entity (LLC or S-Corp)

Tax Rates:

  • Corporate tax rate: 21%
  • Personal tax rate on dividends: 20%
  • Personal tax rate on pass-through income: 17.1% (effective rate including self-employment tax considerations)
C-CORPORATION: DOUBLE TAXATION LAYER 1: Corporate Tax Business profit: $100,000 Corporate tax (21%): -21,000 ───────────────────────────────────────── After-tax corporate profit: $79,000 LAYER 2: Personal Tax on Dividends Dividends distributed: $79,000 Personal tax on dividends (20%): -15,800 ───────────────────────────────────────── Money in owner's pocket: $63,200 TOTAL TAX BURDEN: Corporate tax: $21,000 Personal tax: +$15,800 ───────────────────────────────────────── Total taxes paid: $36,800 EFFECTIVE TAX RATE: 36.8%
PASS-THROUGH ENTITY: SINGLE TAXATION NO CORPORATE TAX LAYER Business profit: $100,000 Corporate tax: $0 ───────────────────────────────────────── Profit flows to owner: $100,000 PERSONAL TAX ONLY Pass-through income: $100,000 Personal tax (17.1%): -17,100 ───────────────────────────────────────── Money in owner's pocket: $82,900 TOTAL TAX BURDEN: $17,100 EFFECTIVE TAX RATE: 17.1%

The Comparison

Entity Type Total Tax Paid Owner Keeps Effective Rate
C-Corporation $36,800 $63,200 36.8%
Pass-Through $17,100 $82,900 17.1%
Difference +$19,700 more tax -$19,700 less money +19.7 percentage points

Bottom Line: C-Corps pay more than double the tax rate of pass-through entities!

How to Calculate This on the Exam

For C-Corporation (Two Layers):

  1. Step 1: Calculate corporate tax
    • Profit × 21% = corporate tax paid
  2. Step 2: Calculate what's left after corporate tax
    • Profit - corporate tax = amount available for dividends
  3. Step 3: Calculate personal tax on dividends
    • Dividends × 20% = personal tax paid
  4. Step 4: Add both tax layers together
    • Corporate tax + personal tax = total tax burden

For Pass-Through Entity (One Layer):

  1. Step 1: Calculate personal tax
    • Profit × 17.1% = total tax burden
  2. That's it—there's no corporate layer!

Pro tip: Show your work step by step. Professors want to see your calculation process, not just the final answer.

Why Choose C-Corp Despite Higher Taxes?

The Paradox

C-Corporations pay nearly double the tax, yet most venture-backed startups still choose them. Why would anyone accept this tax disadvantage?

Why VCs Require C-Corporations

1. Preferred Stock Required

  • VCs demand preferred stock with liquidation preferences, anti-dilution protection, and board rights
  • S-Corporations can only have ONE class of stock (no preferred/common distinction)
  • LLCs technically can create different membership interests, but it's messy and non-standard
  • C-Corporations easily support multiple classes of stock

2. Unlimited Investors

  • VCs plan for multiple funding rounds with new investors each time
  • S-Corporations limited to 100 shareholders maximum
  • C-Corporations have no shareholder limit
  • Allows for Series A, B, C, D... with new investors each round

3. Foreign Investors

  • Many VC funds have foreign limited partners or want to bring in international investors
  • S-Corporations cannot have foreign shareholders (US citizens/residents only)
  • C-Corporations can have foreign investors without restriction

4. Exit Strategy (IPO or Acquisition)

  • Public companies must be C-Corporations
  • If planning IPO, need to be C-Corp from the start
  • Most acquirers prefer buying C-Corps (simpler, more familiar)
  • Converting from S-Corp to C-Corp right before exit creates tax complications

5. Stock Options for Employees

  • C-Corporations can easily grant stock options as employee compensation
  • Stock options in S-Corps and LLCs are complex and problematic
  • High-growth startups need to attract talent with equity

The Trade-Off in Practice

For a High-Growth Startup (VC-Backed):

  • Early years: Usually losing money, so no profit to tax anyway
  • Growth phase: Reinvesting all profit into growth—not taking dividends
  • Exit: Sell company or go public—founders cash out via capital gains, not dividends
  • Result: Double taxation largely avoided because profits aren't distributed as dividends

For a Small Profitable Business (Bakery, Consulting Firm):

  • Owners want to take profits home regularly
  • Not seeking VC funding
  • Not planning to go public
  • Tax burden matters enormously
  • Result: Choose LLC or S-Corp to avoid double taxation and keep more money

When S-Corp Doesn't Work

Scenarios Where Pass-Through Taxation ≠ S-Corp

Sometimes founders want pass-through taxation but S-Corp won't work due to its restrictions:

1. Foreign Co-Founder or Investor

  • S-Corps require all shareholders be US citizens or residents
  • If your co-founder is from another country → can't use S-Corp
  • Solution: Use LLC instead

2. More Than 100 Investors Planned

  • S-Corp caps at 100 shareholders
  • If you anticipate needing more → can't use S-Corp
  • Solution: Use LLC or accept C-Corp taxation

3. Want Different Classes of Equity

  • S-Corp allows only one class of stock
  • Can't give some investors preferred rights over others
  • Solution: Use LLC with different membership interests

4. Corporate Investor Wants In

  • S-Corp cannot have corporations as shareholders
  • If a company wants to invest → can't use S-Corp
  • Solution: Use LLC instead

Other Entity Choice Considerations

Beyond Tax Treatment

Liability Protection

  • C-Corps, S-Corps, and LLCs all provide limited liability
  • Owners not personally liable for business debts (with exceptions for fraud, personal guarantees)
  • Partnerships and sole proprietorships: UNLIMITED personal liability

Formality Requirements

  • C-Corps and S-Corps: Board meetings, minutes, formal corporate structure required
  • LLCs: Much more flexible, less formal
  • Partnerships: Minimal formalities

Cost and Complexity

  • C-Corps: More expensive to set up and maintain (corporate formalities, compliance)
  • LLCs: Simpler and cheaper to maintain
  • Sole proprietorships: Cheapest and simplest

Future Funding Plans

  • Need VC money? → Must be C-Corp
  • Bootstrapping? → LLC or S-Corp saves on taxes
  • Planning crowdfunding with many small investors? → Consider shareholder limits

Exit Strategy

  • Planning IPO? → Must be C-Corp
  • Planning acquisition? → C-Corp preferred by most acquirers
  • Keep forever and take distributions? → Pass-through saves taxes

Factors That Are Usually IRRELEVANT

  • The name of the business
  • What industry you're in (doesn't dictate entity type)
  • Whether founders are friends vs strangers
  • The size of the business in terms of revenue (tax treatment matters more)

Practice Questions

Question 1: Small Business Entity Choice

A client wants to start a small bakery with one partner. They expect modest income for the first few years and want to avoid double taxation. What entity type(s) would you recommend, and why? What factors beyond tax treatment should they consider? Identify at least two. What is one factor that is likely irrelevant to their decision?

Show Sample Answer

Recommendation: LLC or S-Corporation

Why:

  • Both provide pass-through taxation, avoiding the double taxation of C-Corps
  • This means they keep more of their profits (17.1% vs 36.8% effective tax rate)
  • Both provide limited liability protection for the owners
  • For a modest-income bakery, tax savings are significant and they don't need C-Corp features

Other Factors Beyond Tax:

  1. Formality Requirements: S-Corp requires board meetings and corporate minutes; LLC is more flexible with fewer formalities. For a small bakery, LLC's flexibility may be preferable.
  2. Liability Protection: Both LLC and S-Corp shield personal assets from business debts. This is important for a bakery (potential food safety issues, slip-and-fall claims, etc.).
  3. Cost and Complexity: LLC is typically cheaper and simpler to maintain than S-Corp. For a small business, lower administrative burden matters.

Irrelevant Factor:

  • The name of the bakery
  • What types of baked goods they sell
  • Whether the partners are friends or business acquaintances

Question 2: Pass-Through but Not S-Corp

Describe a scenario where a founder wants pass-through taxation, but an S-Corporation would not be a good fit.

Show Sample Answer

Scenario Options (any one is a complete answer):

Option 1: Foreign Co-Founder

  • Founder has a co-founder who is not a US citizen or resident
  • S-Corp requires all shareholders be US citizens/residents
  • Solution: Use LLC instead—gets pass-through taxation and allows foreign owners

Option 2: Many Investors

  • Founder plans to have 150 investors through a crowdfunding campaign
  • S-Corp caps at 100 shareholders
  • Solution: Use LLC instead—no shareholder limit

Option 3: Different Investor Rights

  • Founder wants to give early investors preferred rights (liquidation preference, anti-dilution)
  • S-Corp only allows one class of stock
  • Solution: Use LLC with different membership interests

Option 4: Corporate Investor

  • A corporation wants to invest in the business
  • S-Corp cannot have corporations as shareholders
  • Solution: Use LLC instead

Question 3: Tax Calculation (CRITICAL)

Walk through the two layers of tax that apply to a C-Corporation and explain why the effective tax rate (approximately 36.8%) is significantly higher than for a pass-through entity (approximately 17.1%). Given this tax disadvantage, why might a startup still choose to organize as a C-Corporation?

Show Sample Answer

Part 1: The Two Layers of C-Corp Taxation

Example: $100,000 profit C-CORPORATION: Layer 1 (Corporate Tax): $100,000 × 21% = $21,000 corporate tax $100,000 - $21,000 = $79,000 after corporate tax Layer 2 (Personal Tax): $79,000 × 20% = $15,800 personal tax on dividends $79,000 - $15,800 = $63,200 to owner Total Tax: $21,000 + $15,800 = $36,800 (36.8% effective rate) PASS-THROUGH: Single Layer (Personal Tax Only): $100,000 × 17.1% = $17,100 total tax $100,000 - $17,100 = $82,900 to owner Difference: $36,800 vs $17,100 = C-Corp pays 2.15x more!

Part 2: Why the Higher Rate?

  • C-Corp income is taxed twice: once at corporate level, again when distributed to owners
  • Pass-through income is only taxed once at personal level
  • The double taxation creates an effective rate more than double that of pass-through

Part 3: Why Choose C-Corp Anyway?

  • VCs require preferred stock — S-Corp can only have one class, C-Corp can have multiple
  • Unlimited investors needed — S-Corp caps at 100, C-Corp has no limit for multiple funding rounds
  • Foreign investors — S-Corp requires US citizens only, C-Corp allows foreign investors
  • IPO or acquisition exit — Must be C-Corp for IPO, acquirers prefer C-Corps
  • Stock options for employees — Easier in C-Corp structure
  • Early years have no profit — Startups typically lose money initially, so double taxation doesn't matter yet
  • Don't take dividends — Reinvest profit into growth instead of distributing, avoiding the second tax layer

✓ What's Testable

  • Tax calculation: C-Corp double taxation vs pass-through single taxation
  • Why VCs require C-Corps (preferred stock, unlimited investors, foreign investors, exit strategy)
  • S-Corp limitations (100 shareholders, US only, one class of stock)
  • When to choose LLC vs S-Corp for pass-through taxation
  • Entity choice factors beyond tax (liability, formality, cost, funding plans, exit strategy)

✗ What's NOT Testable

  • Complex S-Corp technical details
  • Detailed tax code provisions beyond basic rates
  • State-specific entity formation rules
