Class 4: VC Finance & Preferred Stock

Understanding venture capital deal structure, valuations, and liquidation preferences

🎧

Class 4 Podcast

Listen first for overview (~12 minutes)

🎯 EXAM FOCUS: The Series B calculation is THE most important calculation on the exam. Master the formula: Existing shares ÷ (1 - New investor %) = Total shares after investment. Liquidation preferences (non-participating vs participating) and when VCs convert are testable. Anti-dilution calculations are NOT tested.

Pre-Money vs Post-Money Valuation

Core Definitions

Pre-Money Valuation: What the company is worth BEFORE the new investment comes in.

Post-Money Valuation: What the company is worth AFTER the new investment is added.

The Fundamental Relationship:

Post-Money Valuation = Pre-Money Valuation + Investment Amount OR (solving from the other direction): Investment Amount ÷ Ownership % = Post-Money Valuation

Simple Example

Scenario: A VC wants to invest $5M for 25% of the company.

Step 1: Calculate Post-Money Valuation If $5M buys 25%, then: $5M ÷ 0.25 = $20M post-money valuation Step 2: Calculate Pre-Money Valuation Post-money - Investment = Pre-money $20M - $5M = $15M pre-money valuation Interpretation: The company was worth $15M before the investment, and will be worth $20M after the investment.

Quick Practice

A VC invests $8M for 40% of a company. What are the pre-money and post-money valuations?

Show Answer
Post-Money = $8M ÷ 0.40 = $20M Pre-Money = $20M - $8M = $12M

The Series B Problem ⭐⭐⭐

🔥 THIS IS THE BIG ONE

This calculation WILL appear on your exam with different numbers. Master the methodology, not just this specific example.

The Setup

Current Capitalization Table:

  • Common stockholders: 10,000,000 shares
  • Series A Preferred investors: 5,000,000 shares (invested $5M at $1.00/share previously)
  • Total existing shares: 15,000,000

Series B Investment Terms:

  • Investment amount: $10,000,000
  • For ownership stake: 50%

Questions to Solve:

  1. How many total shares must be outstanding after Series B closes for them to own exactly 50%?
  2. How many new shares does Series B receive?
  3. What is the price per share for Series B stock?
  4. After Series B closes, what percentage does Common own? Series A?
STEP 1: Calculate Total Shares After Series B The key insight: If Series B owns 50%, then everyone else (Common + Series A) collectively owns the other 50%. We know: - Current shareholders have 15M shares - After the deal, those 15M shares = 50% of total Set up the equation: 15M shares = 50% of Total 15M = 0.5 × Total Total = 15M ÷ 0.5 Total = 30,000,000 shares Answer: 30,000,000 total shares after Series B
STEP 2: Calculate New Shares Issued to Series B Total shares after investment: 30,000,000 Existing shares (before Series B): 15,000,000 ───────────────────────────────────────────── New shares issued: 15,000,000 Answer: Series B receives 15,000,000 new shares
STEP 3: Calculate Price Per Share Series B investment amount: $10,000,000 Series B shares received: 15,000,000 ───────────────────────────────────────── Price per share = $10M ÷ 15M = $0.6667/share Answer: $0.67 per share (rounded) Important Note: This is a DOWN ROUND! Series A paid $1.00/share, Series B pays $0.67/share. The company's valuation decreased between rounds.
STEP 4: Calculate Final Ownership Percentages Total shares outstanding: 30,000,000 Common stockholders: 10,000,000 ÷ 30,000,000 = 33.3% Series A investors: 5,000,000 ÷ 30,000,000 = 16.7% Series B investors: 15,000,000 ÷ 30,000,000 = 50.0% Check (must equal 100%): 33.3% + 16.7% + 50.0% = 100.0% ✓ Final Ownership: - Common: 33.3% - Series A: 16.7% - Series B: 50.0%

The Universal Formula (Use This on the Exam)

Given: Existing shares, Investment amount, Desired ownership %

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Total shares after = Existing shares ÷ (1 - New investor %)
  2. New shares = Total shares after - Existing shares
  3. Price per share = Investment amount ÷ New shares
  4. Each party's % = Their shares ÷ Total shares after

For the Series B problem:

1. Total = 15M ÷ (1 - 0.5) = 15M ÷ 0.5 = 30M 2. New shares = 30M - 15M = 15M 3. Price = $10M ÷ 15M = $0.67 4. Common % = 10M ÷ 30M = 33.3%, etc.

Liquidation Preferences

What Is a Liquidation Preference?

Definition: A term in preferred stock that ensures VCs get paid back their investment (or a multiple of it) BEFORE common stockholders receive anything when the company is sold or liquidated.

Why VCs Demand This:

  • Downside protection: If the exit is disappointing, VCs at least recover their investment
  • Common stockholders bear more of the downside risk
  • Creates a "floor" on VC returns

Common Multiples:

  • 1X: Get investment back (most common)
  • 1.5X or 2X: Get multiple of investment back (more aggressive)

Types of Liquidation Preferences

1. Non-Participating Preferred

At exit, preferred investor chooses the BETTER of:

  • Option A: Take liquidation preference (get 1X, 1.5X, or 2X investment back), OR
  • Option B: Convert to common stock and take pro-rata ownership share

Key Point: Investor picks whichever option pays more.

2. Participating Preferred

At exit, preferred investor gets BOTH:

  • First: Liquidation preference back, AND THEN
  • Second: Pro-rata share of remaining proceeds

Result: Much better for VCs (they "double dip"), much worse for founders.

Non-Participating Preferred: Detailed Example

Investment Terms:

  • VC invested: $1,000,000
  • Pre-money valuation: $4,000,000
  • Post-money valuation: $5,000,000
  • VC ownership: 20% ($1M ÷ $5M)
  • Liquidation preference: 1.5X (VC gets $1.5M preference)

Scenario 1: Company Sells for $2 Million

Option A - Take Liquidation Preference: VC gets: $1,500,000 (their 1.5X preference) Common gets: $500,000 (what's left: $2M - $1.5M) Option B - Convert to Common and Take Pro-Rata: VC gets: $2M × 20% = $400,000 Common gets: $2M × 80% = $1,600,000 VC Decision: Choose Option A $1,500,000 > $400,000 Final Distribution: VC: $1,500,000 Common: $500,000

Scenario 2: Company Sells for $10 Million

Option A - Take Liquidation Preference: VC gets: $1,500,000 Common gets: $8,500,000 ($10M - $1.5M) Option B - Convert to Common: VC gets: $10M × 20% = $2,000,000 Common gets: $10M × 80% = $8,000,000 VC Decision: Choose Option B $2,000,000 > $1,500,000 Final Distribution: VC: $2,000,000 Common: $8,000,000

The Pattern:

  • Low exit price: VCs take the liquidation preference (downside protection kicks in)
  • High exit price: VCs convert to common stock (participate in upside)

Anti-Dilution Protection

What It Is: Protection for existing investors if the company raises money in a later round at a lower price per share (down round).

When It Matters: Series C price < Series B price triggers anti-dilution for Series B investors.

How It Works:

  • Series B investors receive additional shares to maintain their value
  • Common stockholders (founders) get diluted more to compensate Series B
  • Protects VCs from being "punished" by future down rounds

Who Bears the Cost: Common stockholders (founders) absorb the extra dilution.

Important for Exam: You do NOT need to calculate anti-dilution adjustments. Just know conceptually what it is and who it protects.

Question 3: Liquidation Preferences

Explain what a liquidation preference is and why venture capital investors typically require one. Using the non-participating preferred example from class ($1M investment, $4M pre-money valuation, 1.5X liquidation preference), explain why the preferred investor would choose NOT to convert when the company sells for $2 million, but WOULD choose to convert when the company sells for $10 million.

Show Sample Answer

What a Liquidation Preference Is:

A liquidation preference is a term in preferred stock that ensures VCs get paid back their investment (or a multiple of it) BEFORE common stockholders receive anything when the company exits through sale or liquidation.

Why VCs Require It:

  • Downside protection: If the company sells for less than expected, VCs at least recover their investment (or a multiple)
  • Reduces risk for VCs by creating a payment priority
  • Common stockholders (founders) bear more downside risk

Analysis of the Example:

Setup: $1M invested at $4M pre-money ($5M post-money), so VC owns 20%, with 1.5X liquidation preference ($1.5M)

At $2M Exit - DON'T Convert:

Option A (Take preference): $1,500,000 Option B (Convert to 20%): $2M × 20% = $400,000 VC chooses Option A: $1,500,000 > $400,000

At a low exit price, the liquidation preference is worth more than the pro-rata ownership share. The VC uses downside protection.

At $10M Exit - DO Convert:

Option A (Take preference): $1,500,000 Option B (Convert to 20%): $10M × 20% = $2,000,000 VC chooses Option B: $2,000,000 > $1,500,000

At a high exit price, the pro-rata ownership share is worth more than the fixed liquidation preference. The VC converts to participate in the upside.

The Pattern: VCs choose whichever option pays more—preference at low exits (downside protection), conversion at high exits (upside participation).

Question 4: Anti-Dilution Protection

Suppose that after the $10,000,000 Series B investment, the company needs to raise a Series C. Suppose that the Series B investor has anti-dilution protection. At what valuation would the anti-dilution protection become relevant? Who would bear the cost?

Show Answer

When Anti-Dilution Becomes Relevant:

Anti-dilution protection kicks in when the Series C price per share is LOWER than the Series B price per share. Since Series B paid $0.67/share, anti-dilution would trigger if Series C is priced below $0.67/share.

Example: If Series C investors pay $0.50/share, that's a down round—the company's value decreased.

Who Bears the Cost:

  • Common stockholders (founders) bear the cost
  • Series B investors receive additional shares to compensate for the down round
  • These additional shares come from diluting the common stockholders more heavily
  • Series B's ownership percentage stays the same or increases, while founders' percentage decreases more than it would have without anti-dilution protection

Note: You do NOT need to calculate the specific anti-dilution adjustment for the exam—just understand the concept and who it protects.
