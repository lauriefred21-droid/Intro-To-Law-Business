Class 1: Financial Statements

Understanding how companies measure value, track cash, and report financial performance

🎧

Class 1 Podcast

Listen first for overview (~12 minutes)

🎯 EXAM FOCUS: The cupcake problem (accrual vs cash accounting) WILL appear with different numbers. Balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement basics are testable. Depreciation/amortization calculations are NOT tested.

Book Value vs Market Value

Core Distinction

Book Value: Assets minus liabilities as recorded on the balance sheet at historical cost.

Market Value: What investors actually believe the company is worth (stock price × outstanding shares).

Why the gap exists: Balance sheets only capture tangible assets at the price originally paid. They don't reflect intangible assets (brand value, patents, customer relationships, trade secrets) or future earnings potential.

📊 Example: Coca-Cola

Book Value: Relatively Low

  • Coca-Cola franchises most of its bottling operations
  • Physical assets (factories, equipment) are on franchisees' books, not Coke's
  • Balance sheet shows limited tangible assets

Market Value: Massive ($250B+ market cap)

  • Brand worth estimated at $80B+
  • Secret formula (intangible asset, not on balance sheet)
  • Global distribution network and customer loyalty
  • Future earnings from these intangibles drive market value

The takeaway: Coca-Cola's value is in its BRAND and FORMULA, not in physical assets. Book value fundamentally cannot capture this.

Accrual Accounting vs Cash Accounting

The Fundamental Difference

Cash Accounting: Record revenue when cash received, expenses when cash paid

  • Simple and straightforward
  • Matches your bank account
  • Good for very small businesses

Accrual Accounting: Record revenue when earned, expenses when incurred

  • Required by GAAP for most companies
  • Better matches revenues with related expenses
  • Shows economic reality, not just cash flow

🔥 The Cupcake Problem (CRITICAL FOR EXAM)

Scenario: You start a cupcake business and complete these transactions in Q1:

  1. Deposit $3,000 in business bank account
  2. Purchase an oven for $1,000 cash
  3. Purchase ingredients for $500 cash
  4. Receive $800 advance payment for a wedding order you'll fulfill in Q2

Question 1: How much cash do you have at the end of Q1?

Starting cash: $3,000 Purchase oven: -$1,000 Purchase ingredients: -$500 Receive advance: +$800 ──────────────────────────────── CASH ON HAND: $2,300

Question 2: How much revenue have you recognized in Q1?

Revenue Analysis: $800 advance payment: - Have you EARNED it yet? NO - Have you delivered the cupcakes? NO - Have you fulfilled the order? NO → This is UNEARNED REVENUE (a liability) → You owe the customer cupcakes or a refund TOTAL Q1 REVENUE: $0

Key Insight: Cash = $2,300 but Revenue = $0. The $800 sits on the balance sheet as a liability (unearned revenue) until you deliver the cupcakes in Q2. This is the essence of accrual accounting: revenue recognition is about EARNING, not RECEIVING.

The Three Financial Statements

1. Balance Sheet

Core Question: What does the company own, and what does it owe?

Formula: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Timing: Snapshot at a single point in time (like a photograph)

ASSETS Current Assets (cash, inventory, receivables) Fixed Assets (PP&E - property, plant, equipment) LIABILITIES Current Liabilities (due within 1 year) Long-term Liabilities (debt, bonds) EQUITY Shareholder equity (assets - liabilities)

2. Income Statement (P&L)

Core Question: Is the company profitable?

Timing: Performance over a period (quarter, year)

Revenue (top line) - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D) = Operating Income (EBIT) - Interest & Taxes = Net Income (bottom line)

Note: This uses accrual accounting. "Revenue" is what you've earned, not necessarily what cash you've collected.

3. Cash Flow Statement

Core Question: Where did the actual cash come from and where did it go?

Why it matters: You can be profitable on the income statement but still run out of cash.

CASH FROM OPERATIONS Net income + Adjustments (depreciation, changes in working capital) CASH FROM INVESTING - Capital expenditures (buying equipment) +/- Asset sales CASH FROM FINANCING + Debt raised / equity issued - Debt repaid / dividends paid

Example of the gap: A fast-growing company might be profitable but burning cash because customers pay slowly (receivables) while suppliers demand immediate payment.

Practice Questions

Question 1: Cash vs Revenue

A law firm starts operations in January and:

  • Receives $50,000 retainer from a client for work to be performed over 6 months
  • Completes $8,000 worth of work in January
  • Pays $3,000 for January office rent
  • Receives $2,000 bill for legal research services (will pay next month)

What should the firm recognize as January revenue under accrual accounting?

Show Answer

Answer: $8,000

Explanation:

  • The $50,000 retainer is unearned revenue (liability) until work is performed
  • Only $8,000 of work was actually completed in January
  • Therefore, only $8,000 is recognized as revenue
  • The remaining $42,000 stays as a liability on the balance sheet

Cash received ≠ Revenue earned. This is the core of accrual accounting.

Question 2: Book vs Market Value

Company X has the following:

  • Physical assets (buildings, equipment): $10M
  • Liabilities: $4M
  • Book value: $6M
  • Market capitalization: $50M

Explain why market value exceeds book value by such a large margin. What types of assets might account for this difference?

Show Answer

Explanation:

The $44M gap between book value ($6M) and market value ($50M) likely reflects intangible assets not captured on the balance sheet:

  • Brand value: Customer recognition and loyalty
  • Intellectual property: Patents, trademarks, proprietary technology
  • Human capital: Skilled workforce, management expertise
  • Customer relationships: Long-term contracts, recurring revenue
  • Market position: Network effects, competitive moat
  • Future earnings potential: Expected growth and profitability

Balance sheets use historical cost and only record tangible assets. Market value reflects what investors believe all assets—tangible and intangible—are worth based on future cash flows.

Question 3: Three Statements

A company reports net income of $1M but cash from operations of -$500k (negative). How is this possible? Provide two specific examples of transactions that could cause this gap.

Show Answer

Answer: This is possible because the income statement uses accrual accounting while the cash flow statement tracks actual cash movements.

Example 1: Accounts Receivable Increase

  • Company makes $2M in sales (recognized as revenue)
  • But customers haven't paid yet (sales on credit)
  • Income statement shows the revenue, cash flow statement shows no cash received

Example 2: Inventory Build-Up

  • Company spends $1M cash buying inventory
  • Inventory doesn't become an expense until sold
  • Cash goes out immediately, but expense is deferred

Key Insight: Growing companies often show this pattern—profitable but cash-burning because they're investing in receivables and inventory faster than they're collecting cash.

✓ What's Testable

  • Accrual vs cash accounting (especially revenue recognition)
  • Book value vs market value concept and why they differ
  • Basic structure of balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement
  • How the three statements relate to each other

✗ What's NOT Testable

  • Detailed depreciation or amortization calculations
  • Complex tax accounting
  • GAAP technical rules beyond basic recognition
