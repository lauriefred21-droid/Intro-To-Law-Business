The Four Main Entity Types

1. C-Corporation

Taxation: Double taxation (corporate + personal)

Key Features:

  • Can have unlimited shareholders
  • Can have different classes of stock (preferred + common)
  • Foreign investors allowed
  • Required for VC-backed startups

Best For: High-growth startups seeking VC funding

2. S-Corporation

Taxation: Pass-through (only personal tax)

Key Features:

  • Maximum 100 shareholders
  • Only US citizens/residents as shareholders
  • Only ONE class of stock allowed
  • No corporate-level tax

Best For: Small businesses with modest growth, US-only investors

3. LLC (Limited Liability Company)

Taxation: Pass-through (by default)

Key Features:

  • No limit on number of members
  • Foreign investors allowed
  • Very flexible operating agreement
  • Can choose to be taxed as C-Corp if desired

Best For: Flexible structure, real estate, partnerships

4. Partnership / Sole Proprietorship

Taxation: Pass-through

Key Features:

  • No liability protection (general partnership)
  • Simplest to set up
  • Partners personally liable for business debts

Best For: Very small businesses, service providers

The Tax Calculation You MUST Know

🔥 This calculation WILL appear on the exam! Practice with different profit amounts.

The Scenario

A business earns $100,000 in profit.

Compare the total tax burden under:

  1. C-Corporation (double taxation)
  2. Pass-through entity (LLC or S-Corp)

Assumptions:

  • Corporate tax rate: 21%
  • Personal tax rate on dividends: 20%
  • Personal tax rate on pass-through income: 17.1% (includes self-employment tax consideration)

C-CORPORATION TAXATION

LAYER 1: Corporate Tax
Business profit:           $100,000
Corporate tax (21%):       -$21,000
─────────────────────────────────
After-tax profit:           $79,000

LAYER 2: Personal Tax on Dividends
Dividends received:         $79,000
Personal tax (20%):        -$15,800
─────────────────────────────────
Money in your pocket:       $63,200

TOTAL TAX PAID:
Corporate tax:              $21,000
Personal tax:              +$15,800
─────────────────────────────────
Total:                      $36,800

EFFECTIVE TAX RATE: 36.8%

PASS-THROUGH TAXATION (LLC or S-Corp)

NO CORPORATE TAX
Business profit:           $100,000
Corporate tax:                  $0
─────────────────────────────────
Profit flows to owner:     $100,000

PERSONAL TAX
Pass-through income:       $100,000
Personal tax (17.1%):      -$17,100
─────────────────────────────────
Money in your pocket:       $82,900

TOTAL TAX PAID: $17,100

EFFECTIVE TAX RATE: 17.1%

The Comparison

Entity Type Total Tax Money You Keep Effective Rate
C-Corp $36,800 $63,200 36.8%
Pass-Through $17,100 $82,900 17.1%
Difference +$19,700 MORE tax -$19,700 LESS money +19.7% higher rate

C-Corps pay MORE THAN DOUBLE the tax!

How to Ace This on the Exam

  1. C-Corp: Calculate TWO layers
    • Step 1: Profit × 21% = corporate tax
    • Step 2: (Profit - corporate tax) × 20% = personal tax
    • Step 3: Add them together
  2. Pass-Through: ONE layer
    • Profit × 17.1% = total tax
  3. Show your work! Professor wants to see the calculation steps

Why Choose a C-Corp Despite Higher Taxes?

The Paradox

C-Corps pay nearly DOUBLE the tax, yet most VC-backed startups still choose them. Why?

Reasons VCs Require C-Corps:

  1. Preferred Stock
    • VCs want preferred stock with special rights
    • S-Corps can only have ONE class of stock
    • LLCs technically can, but it's messy
    • C-Corps easily support multiple stock classes
  2. Unlimited Investors
    • VCs want to bring in more investors later
    • S-Corps limited to 100 shareholders
    • C-Corps have no limit
  3. Foreign Investors
    • VCs often have foreign LPs or want foreign investors
    • S-Corps can't have foreign shareholders
    • C-Corps can
  4. Easier Exit (IPO or Acquisition)
    • Public companies must be C-Corps
    • Acquirers prefer buying C-Corps
    • Converting S-Corp to C-Corp right before exit creates tax issues
  5. Stock Options for Employees
    • C-Corps can easily grant stock options
    • S-Corp/LLC stock options are complicated

The Trade-Off in Practice

For a high-growth startup:

  • Early years: Usually no profit (losing money), so tax doesn't matter
  • Later years: If profitable, founders accept the tax hit because:
  • → They raised VC money (couldn't do that without C-Corp)
  • → They're heading toward exit (IPO or acquisition)
  • → The business grew way bigger than it could have without VC

For a small business (bakery, consulting firm):

  • Want to keep profits → tax matters a LOT
  • Don't need VC money
  • Not planning IPO
  • → Choose LLC or S-Corp to save on taxes

When S-Corp Doesn't Work

Scenarios Where Pass-Through Taxation ≠ S-Corp

Founder wants pass-through taxation BUT S-Corp doesn't work if:

  1. Foreign co-founder or investor
    • S-Corps require all shareholders be US citizens/residents
    • → Use LLC instead
  2. More than 100 investors planned
    • S-Corp caps at 100 shareholders
    • → Use LLC instead
  3. Want different classes of stock
    • S-Corp only allows one class
    • → Use LLC with different membership interests
  4. Corporate investor wants in
    • S-Corp can't have corporations as shareholders
    • → Use LLC instead

Other Entity Choice Factors

Beyond Tax Treatment

Factors to Consider:

  1. Liability Protection
    • All corps and LLCs provide limited liability
    • Partnerships/sole proprietors have UNLIMITED personal liability
  2. Formality Requirements
    • C-Corps: Board meetings, minutes, formal structure
    • S-Corps: Same as C-Corp
    • LLCs: Flexible, less formal
  3. Cost and Complexity
    • C-Corps: More expensive to set up and maintain
    • LLCs: Simpler and cheaper
  4. Future Funding Plans
    • Need VC money? → Must be C-Corp
    • Bootstrapping? → LLC or S-Corp fine
  5. Exit Strategy
    • Planning IPO? → Must be C-Corp
    • Planning acquisition? → C-Corp preferred by acquirers
    • Keep forever? → Pass-through saves taxes

Likely IRRELEVANT Factors:

  • The name of the business (has nothing to do with entity type)
  • What industry you're in (doesn't dictate entity choice)
  • Whether founders are friends vs strangers

Review Questions (From Professor)

Question 1

A client comes to you and says she wants to start a small bakery with one partner. They expect modest income for the first few years and want to avoid double taxation. What entity type(s) would you recommend, and why? What factors beyond tax treatment should they consider when choosing an entity? Identify at least two. What is one factor that is likely irrelevant to their decision?

Your Answer Should Include:

  • Recommendation: LLC or S-Corp (both provide pass-through taxation)
  • Why: Avoids double taxation, keeps more profit, provides liability protection
  • Other factors to consider:
    1. Liability protection (both LLC and S-Corp provide this)
    2. Formality requirements (LLC is more flexible, S-Corp requires corporate formalities)
    3. Future funding plans (if they ever want investors, may need to convert)
  • Irrelevant factor: Name of the business, what baked goods they sell, whether partners are friends

Question 2

Describe a scenario where a founder wants pass-through taxation, but an S-Corp would not be a good fit.

Your Answer (pick one scenario):

  • Founder has a co-founder who is not a US citizen → Can't use S-Corp, use LLC instead
  • Founder wants to bring in corporate investors → S-Corp doesn't allow corporate shareholders
  • Founder plans to have 150 investors eventually → S-Corp caps at 100 shareholders
  • Founder wants to give different rights to different investors → S-Corp only allows one class of stock

Question 3 (THE TAX CALCULATION)

Walk through the two layers of tax that apply to a C-Corporation and explain why the effective tax rate (approximately 32.9%/36.8%) is significantly higher than for a pass-through entity (approximately 17.1%). Given this tax disadvantage, why might a startup still choose to organize as a C-Corporation?

ANSWER - Part 1: The Calculation 
C-CORP (on $100k profit):
Layer 1: $100k × 21% = $21k corporate tax
After corp tax: $79k remains
Layer 2: $79k × 20% = $15.8k personal tax
Total tax: $21k + $15.8k = $36.8k (36.8% rate)

PASS-THROUGH (on $100k profit):
One layer: $100k × 17.1% = $17.1k
Total tax: $17.1k (17.1% rate)

Difference: C-Corp pays 2x more!

Part 2: Why Choose C-Corp Anyway?

  • VCs require preferred stock (S-Corp can't have multiple classes)
  • Need unlimited investors for future rounds (S-Corp caps at 100)
  • Want foreign investors (S-Corp requires US citizens only)
  • Planning IPO or acquisition (must be C-Corp)
  • Need stock options for employees (easier in C-Corp)
  • Early years have no profit anyway (so double taxation doesn't matter yet)

  • Remember: C-Corp = TWO layers, Pass-through = ONE layer
  • S-Corp limitations: 100 shareholders, US only, one class of stock
  • Why C-Corp despite taxes: VCs require it for preferred stock + unlimited investors
