Classes 8-9: Mergers & Acquisitions

🎯 Focus: Strategic vs financial acquirers, why premiums exist, poison pills, stock collars

Strategic vs Financial Acquirers

Strategic Acquirers

Who they are: Operating companies in same or related industry

Why they buy companies:

  • Synergies: 1 + 1 = 3 (combined worth more than separate)
  • Market expansion: Enter new geographies or customer segments
  • Eliminate competition: Buy a competitor
  • Acquire technology/IP: Buy innovation instead of building it
  • Vertical integration: Buy supplier or distributor

Examples:

  • Facebook buying Instagram (eliminate competitor, acquire talent/users)
  • Disney buying Pixar (acquire content and creative talent)
  • Amazon buying Whole Foods (enter grocery retail)

Can pay premium because: Synergies create extra value

Financial Acquirers (Private Equity)

Who they are: PE firms, investment funds

Why they buy companies:

  • Financial engineering: Use leverage (debt) to amplify returns
  • Operational improvements: Cut costs, improve management
  • Portfolio diversification: Spread risk across multiple companies
  • Exit in 3-7 years: Flip for profit via IPO or sale

How they create value:

  • Use lots of debt (leverage magnifies equity returns)
  • Improve operations and cut costs
  • Provide management expertise
  • Sell at higher multiple later

Examples:

  • KKR buying RJR Nabisco (famous 1980s LBO)
  • Blackstone buying Hilton Hotels
  • Vista Equity buying software companies

Can't pay as much because: No operating synergies, rely on financial engineering

Key Differences

Aspect Strategic Financial (PE)
Holding Period Forever (integrate) 3-7 years (flip it)
Value Source Synergies Leverage + ops improvements
Price Premium Higher (pay for synergies) Lower (no synergies)
Integration Deep (merge operations) Light (keep independent)
Management Often replace Often keep/incentivize

Why Acquisition Premiums Exist

What Is a Premium?

Definition: Amount paid above current stock price

Typical premium: 20-40% above pre-announcement stock price

Example:

  • Target company trading at $50/share
  • Acquirer offers $65/share
  • Premium = $15/share = 30%

Why Premiums Are Paid

1. Control Premium

Buying the whole company gives you CONTROL - worth more than just owning shares

  • Can change strategy
  • Replace management
  • Merge operations
  • Sell off pieces

2. Synergies

Combined company worth more than two separate companies

  • Cost synergies: Eliminate duplicate functions (two HR departments → one)
  • Revenue synergies: Cross-sell products, bigger market share
  • Operational synergies: Shared technology, economies of scale

3. Market Undervaluation

Acquirer believes stock price is too low (market doesn't see full value)

4. Competition Among Bidders

Multiple acquirers drive up price in bidding war

5. Target Board Needs to Recommend

Board won't recommend deal unless shareholders get premium

Discount Rates and Valuation

Key Concept: Future cash flows are worth less today (time value of money)

Discount rate = Required rate of return

Who has lower discount rates:

  • Large public companies: Can borrow cheap, diversified, stable
  • Strategic acquirers: Lower risk (synergies reduce uncertainty)

Who has higher discount rates:

  • Startups: High risk, uncertain future
  • PE firms: Use lots of debt (debt is risky)

Why this matters for M&A:

  • Lower discount rate → higher valuation
  • Strategic acquirer with low discount rate values target higher than PE firm
  • This is why strategics often outbid PE firms

Takeover Defenses

Poison Pill (Shareholder Rights Plan)

What it is: Defensive mechanism that makes hostile takeover extremely expensive

How it works:

  1. Board adopts poison pill (usually as prevention, before any threat)
  2. If hostile acquirer buys >10-20% of shares (trigger)
  3. ALL OTHER shareholders get right to buy shares at huge discount (like 50% off)
  4. This massively dilutes the hostile acquirer
  5. Makes takeover prohibitively expensive

Example:

  • Hostile acquirer buys 15% of company for $1 billion
  • Poison pill triggers
  • Other shareholders can now buy shares at 50% discount
  • They exercise these rights, doubling number of shares
  • Acquirer's 15% becomes 7.5%, and they paid full price!

Purpose: Force hostile acquirer to negotiate with board

Other Takeover Defenses

Staggered Board

Only 1/3 of board elected each year → takes 2 years to gain control

Supermajority Voting

Require 2/3 or 3/4 vote to approve merger (not just 50%+1)

Golden Parachutes

Huge severance for executives if company is acquired → makes deal more expensive

Crown Jewel Defense

Sell off best assets to make company less attractive

White Knight

Find friendly acquirer to outbid hostile one

Deal Mechanics

Stock vs Cash Deals

Cash Deal

What happens: Acquirer pays cash for target shares

Target shareholders: Get cash, walk away

Pros for acquirer: Clean, simple, target shareholders gone

Cons for acquirer: Need to raise cash (debt or sell assets)

Stock Deal

What happens: Acquirer gives its own stock in exchange for target shares

Target shareholders: Become shareholders of combined company

Pros for acquirer: Don't need cash

Cons for acquirer: Dilutes existing shareholders

Risk in stock deals: Stock price can change between announcement and close

Stock Collar

Purpose: Protect both parties from stock price volatility in stock deals

How it works:

  • Set floor and ceiling on exchange ratio
  • If acquirer stock goes up a lot → target gets fewer shares (collar caps their gain)
  • If acquirer stock goes down a lot → target gets more shares (collar limits their loss)

Example:

Deal: Target shareholders get $50 worth of acquirer stock
Collar: 0.8 to 1.2 shares per target share

Acquirer stock at announcement: $50/share
→ Exchange ratio: 1.0 share per target share

Scenario 1: Acquirer stock rises to $75
Without collar: 0.67 shares (target loses out)
With collar: 0.8 shares (floor protects target)

Scenario 2: Acquirer stock falls to $30
Without collar: 1.67 shares (acquirer pays more)
With collar: 1.2 shares (ceiling protects acquirer)

Material Adverse Change (MAC) Clause

What it is: Lets acquirer walk away if something really bad happens to target

Examples of MAC:

  • Major customer leaves
  • Key technology obsoleted
  • Large lawsuit filed
  • Fraud discovered

NOT considered MAC:

  • General economic downturn
  • Industry-wide problems
  • Normal business fluctuations

Very hard to invoke - courts interpret narrowly

Break-Up Fees

What it is: Target pays acquirer if deal falls apart (usually 2-4% of deal value)

When target pays:

  • Target accepts competing bid
  • Target shareholders vote down the deal
  • Target board changes recommendation

Purpose: Compensate acquirer for diligence costs and time

💡 Exam Tips for M&A

  • Strategic vs Financial: Strategic pays more (synergies), PE uses leverage
  • Premiums exist because: Control value + synergies + undervaluation
  • Poison pill: Triggers massive dilution if hostile acquirer crosses threshold
  • Stock collar: Sets floor and ceiling on exchange ratio
  • Discount rates: Lower rate = higher valuation (strategics < PE firms)
❌ NOT TESTED: Complex valuation calculations (DCF, etc.), detailed merger agreement structure
