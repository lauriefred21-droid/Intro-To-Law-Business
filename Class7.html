Class 7: Mergers & Acquisitions

Understanding buyer types, deal structures, and strategic considerations in M&A

🎧

Class 7 Podcast

Listen first for overview (~12 minutes)

🎯 EXAM FOCUS: Know the difference between strategic and financial buyers (who pays more and why). Understand stock vs asset purchases and when each is preferred. Know what earnouts are and why they're used. Exchange ratio calculations and detailed merger mechanics are NOT tested.

Strategic vs Financial Buyers

Strategic Buyers

Who They Are: Operating companies in the same or related industry buying to integrate the target into their business.

Examples:

  • Google buying YouTube
  • Facebook buying Instagram
  • Amazon buying Whole Foods
  • A pharmaceutical company buying a biotech startup

Why They Buy:

  • Synergies: 1 + 1 = 3 (combined company worth more than sum of parts)
  • Eliminate competition: Remove a competitor from the market
  • Vertical integration: Control more of the supply chain
  • Acquire technology or talent: Get capabilities faster than building in-house
  • Enter new markets: Geographic or product expansion
  • Economies of scale: Spread fixed costs over larger revenue base

How They Create Value:

  • Revenue synergies (cross-sell products, expand distribution)
  • Cost synergies (eliminate duplicate functions, bulk purchasing)
  • Strategic positioning (market share, competitive advantage)

Financial Buyers

Who They Are: Private equity firms and investment funds buying to generate financial returns, not to integrate into an operating business.

Examples:

  • KKR, Blackstone, Carlyle Group
  • Buying portfolio companies to hold for 3-7 years then sell
  • Not operating the business themselves—hire management team

Why They Buy:

  • Financial return: Buy low, improve, sell high
  • Portfolio diversification: Spread risk across multiple companies
  • Operational improvements: Professionalize management, cut costs
  • Leverage: Use debt to amplify returns

How They Create Value:

  • Operational improvements (better management, efficiency gains)
  • Financial engineering (optimize capital structure, use leverage)
  • Strategic repositioning (refocus on core business, shed underperforming units)
  • Multiple arbitrage (buy at low multiple, sell at higher multiple)

Who Pays More?

General Rule: Strategic buyers typically pay more than financial buyers

Why Strategic Buyers Pay More:

  • Can realize synergies that financial buyers can't (revenue growth from combined customer base, cost savings from eliminating duplicates)
  • Willing to pay for strategic value beyond pure financials (eliminating a competitor, preventing competitor from acquiring target)
  • Can use their own stock as currency (doesn't require raising cash)
  • May face competitive pressure (if we don't buy them, our rival will)

Why Financial Buyers Pay Less:

  • Can only realize standalone value (no synergies with other businesses)
  • Disciplined return requirements (need specific IRR, won't overpay)
  • Limited by debt capacity (can only borrow so much to finance deal)
  • Must exit in 3-7 years (can't justify paying for very long-term value)

Exception: In auction processes with multiple bidders, financial buyers sometimes win by being faster, more certain, or less demanding on due diligence.

Stock Purchase vs Asset Purchase

Stock Purchase

What It Is: Buyer purchases the target company's shares directly from shareholders. Buyer owns the entire company including all assets, liabilities, and legal history.

Structure:

  • Buyer acquires shares from target's shareholders
  • Target company continues to exist (same legal entity)
  • Buyer becomes new owner of the company
  • All contracts, licenses, and obligations transfer automatically

Advantages for Buyer:

  • Simplicity: One transaction, no need to transfer individual assets
  • Contracts stay intact: Licenses, permits, customer contracts transfer automatically
  • Employees transfer automatically: No need to rehire everyone
  • Minority squeeze-out: Can often force minority shareholders to sell

Disadvantages for Buyer:

  • Inherit all liabilities: Known and unknown (lawsuits, environmental, tax issues)
  • Can't cherry-pick: Must take everything (good and bad assets)
  • No step-up in tax basis: Can't depreciate assets to reduce taxes
  • Due diligence risk: Hidden liabilities may exist

When Used:

  • Target has valuable contracts that are hard to transfer
  • Target has licenses or permits tied to the corporate entity
  • Target is clean with limited liability concerns
  • Many shareholders (easier than getting everyone to agree to asset sale)

Asset Purchase

What It Is: Buyer purchases specific assets from the target company. Target company remains intact but sells off its assets.

Structure:

  • Buyer selects which assets to buy
  • Seller retains corporate entity and any excluded assets/liabilities
  • Each asset must be transferred individually
  • Contracts may need consent to assign

Advantages for Buyer:

  • Cherry-pick assets: Buy what you want, leave what you don't
  • Leave liabilities behind: Generally don't assume seller's liabilities (except assumed ones)
  • Tax benefits: Step-up in asset basis allows higher depreciation
  • Cleaner transaction: Less risk of inheriting problems

Disadvantages for Buyer:

  • Complexity: Must transfer each asset individually
  • Third-party consents: May need consent to transfer contracts, leases, licenses
  • Sales tax: May trigger sales tax on asset transfers
  • Can lose key contracts: If counterparty won't consent to assignment

When Used:

  • Target has significant liabilities buyer wants to avoid
  • Buyer wants only specific assets (certain product lines, facilities)
  • Target is in bankruptcy (asset sale preferred)
  • Buyer wants tax benefits from stepped-up basis

Stock vs Asset: Quick Comparison

Factor Stock Purchase Asset Purchase
Liabilities Buyer inherits all Buyer generally avoids

Cherry-Pick No Yes

Earnouts

What Is an Earnout?

Definition: Part of the purchase price is contingent on the target achieving certain performance metrics post-closing (typically revenue, EBITDA, or product milestones).

Structure:

  • Base payment at closing (e.g., $50M upfront)
  • Additional payment if targets met (e.g., up to $20M if revenue exceeds $100M in Year 1)
  • Performance measured over 1-3 years typically

Why Use Earnouts?

Bridge Valuation Gap:

  • Seller thinks company worth $100M
  • Buyer thinks it's worth $70M
  • Solution: $70M upfront + $30M earnout if seller's projections prove correct

Align Incentives:

  • Keep sellers (now employees) motivated post-closing
  • Ensure sellers don't walk away after collecting cash
  • Tie payout to actual performance, not just promises

Reduce Risk for Buyer:

  • Don't pay full price if projections don't materialize
  • Seller shares downside risk if performance disappoints

When Commonly Used:

  • High uncertainty about future performance
  • Target is a startup or early-stage company with unproven projections
  • Seller's management staying on post-acquisition
  • Cyclical business where current earnings may not be sustainable

Common Earnout Issues

1. Metric Definition

  • What exactly counts as "revenue"? (gross vs net, one-time vs recurring)
  • How is EBITDA calculated? (what adjustments allowed?)
  • Must be precisely defined to avoid disputes

2. Buyer's Control

  • Buyer now controls the business and can affect earnout metrics
  • Could shift resources away from target, hurting its performance
  • Could change accounting methods to reduce earnout payment
  • Sellers worry about buyer manipulation

3. Integration vs Standalone Performance

  • If business integrated into buyer's operations, hard to measure standalone performance
  • Shared costs, transferred customers, combined products—who gets credit?

4. Seller Retention

  • Earnouts often require seller to stay as employee
  • What if seller quits or is fired? Do they forfeit earnout?
  • Can create golden handcuffs or hostage situations

Other M&A Considerations

Merger vs Acquisition

Merger: Two companies combine into one, typically with both boards approving and both sets of shareholders voting.

  • Often called "merger of equals" (though rarely truly equal)
  • Target company ceases to exist, merged into buyer
  • Shareholders of both companies become shareholders of combined entity

Acquisition: One company buys another, which may or may not continue to exist separately.

  • Clear buyer and seller
  • Target's shareholders cash out or receive acquirer's stock
  • Target may be kept as subsidiary or fully integrated

Practical Reality: Most "mergers" are actually acquisitions—one company is really in control.

Deal Protections

No-Shop Clause: Seller can't solicit other bids once deal is signed

  • Protects buyer's investment in due diligence
  • Usually allows "fiduciary out" for superior proposals

Breakup Fee: If deal fails under certain conditions, seller pays buyer a fee (typically 2-4% of deal value)

  • Compensates buyer for deal costs if seller walks to superior bid
  • Discourages competing bids (they must be significantly better to justify paying the fee)

Matching Rights: Buyer gets chance to match any superior offer before seller can accept it

Practice Questions

Question 1: Strategic vs Financial Buyers

Explain the difference between strategic and financial buyers. Why do strategic buyers typically pay more for the same target company? Provide a specific example.

Show Sample Answer

Strategic Buyers: Operating companies in the same or related industry that acquire targets to integrate them into their existing business. They seek synergies and strategic benefits.

Financial Buyers: Private equity firms that acquire companies as financial investments, planning to hold for 3-7 years and sell. They focus on standalone financial returns.

Why Strategic Buyers Pay More:

  • Synergies: Can realize revenue synergies (cross-selling, expanded distribution) and cost synergies (eliminating duplicate functions) that financial buyers cannot
  • Strategic value: Willing to pay to eliminate a competitor, acquire key technology, or prevent a rival from acquiring the target
  • Currency: Can use their stock as acquisition currency, not limited to cash
  • Competitive dynamics: May face pressure to act before competitors acquire the target

Example: Facebook's acquisition of Instagram for $1B in 2012. A financial buyer would have valued Instagram based on its standalone financials (minimal revenue at the time). Facebook (strategic buyer) paid significantly more because they saw synergies: integrate Instagram into Facebook's ad platform, leverage Facebook's infrastructure, prevent Google or Twitter from acquiring it, and capture mobile photo-sharing market.

Question 2: Stock vs Asset Purchase

Compare stock purchases and asset purchases from a buyer's perspective. When would a buyer prefer an asset purchase over a stock purchase? What are the key trade-offs?

Show Sample Answer

Stock Purchase: Buyer purchases shares of the target company, acquiring the entire entity with all its assets and liabilities.

Asset Purchase: Buyer purchases specific assets from the target, cherry-picking what to buy and leaving unwanted assets and liabilities behind.

When Buyer Prefers Asset Purchase:

  • Significant liabilities: Target has pending lawsuits, environmental issues, or tax problems buyer wants to avoid
  • Selective acquisition: Buyer wants only certain product lines, facilities, or assets—not the entire business
  • Tax benefits: Asset purchase allows step-up in tax basis, enabling higher depreciation deductions
  • Bankruptcy: Target is in bankruptcy and buyer wants clean acquisition

Key Trade-Offs:

  • Liability protection vs Simplicity: Asset purchase avoids liabilities but is more complex (must transfer each asset individually)
  • Tax benefits vs Contract transfers: Asset purchase provides tax step-up but may require third-party consents for contracts, licenses, permits
  • Flexibility vs Transaction costs: Can cherry-pick assets but higher legal and transaction costs

Question 3: Earnouts

What is an earnout and why are they used in M&A transactions? What are the main challenges with earnouts?

Show Sample Answer

What an Earnout Is: A portion of the purchase price that is contingent on the target company achieving specified performance metrics after closing (typically revenue, EBITDA, or product development milestones measured over 1-3 years).

Why Earnouts Are Used:

  • Bridge valuation gap: When buyer and seller disagree on value, earnout allows seller to get higher price if their optimistic projections prove correct
  • Align incentives: Keep seller's management team motivated and engaged post-closing
  • Reduce buyer's risk: Don't pay full price upfront if performance targets aren't met
  • Handle uncertainty: Appropriate when future performance is genuinely unpredictable (early-stage company, new product, uncertain market)

Main Challenges:

  • Metric definition: Parties often dispute what counts toward earnout (what expenses are deducted? which revenue counts?)
  • Buyer control: Buyer now controls the business and can influence earnout metrics through resource allocation, accounting changes, or integration decisions
  • Integration conflicts: Integrating target into buyer's business makes it hard to measure standalone performance for earnout purposes
  • Retention issues: If seller must stay as employee to earn earnout, creates golden handcuffs and potential for disputes if employment terminates

Bottom Line: Earnouts can be useful tools but require very precise drafting and create potential for post-closing disputes.
