Relational contracts, the BSC/Medinol case, and key contract provisions

Relational Contracts vs Ordinary Contracts

Ordinary Contracts (One-Shot Transactions)

Characteristics:

  • Single transaction: One-time deal, then done
  • Clear terms upfront: Easy to specify price, deliverables, timeline
  • Complete specification possible: Can detail everything in advance
  • Limited relationship value: No ongoing relationship to preserve
  • Dispute approach: If breach occurs, sue and walk away

Examples:

  • Buying a car from a dealer
  • One-time consulting engagement
  • Construction contract for a specific building
  • Purchasing equipment

Relational Contracts (Long-Term Relationships)

Characteristics:

  • Ongoing relationship: Multi-year commitment with repeated interactions
  • Uncertain future: Can't predict all circumstances that will arise
  • Flexibility required: Need ability to adapt as conditions change
  • Relationship-specific investments: Both parties invest resources that only have value in this relationship
  • Dispute approach: If problems arise, work them out (relationship itself has value)
  • Trust essential: Can't contract for everything, must rely on good faith

Examples:

  • Long-term supplier relationships (like BSC/Medinol)
  • Employment agreements
  • Franchise agreements
  • Joint ventures and strategic partnerships

Key Differences

Aspect Ordinary Contract Relational Contract
Duration One-time or short-term Long-term, ongoing
Specificity Detailed, complete terms Flexible framework, gaps inevitable
Adaptation Unnecessary Expected and required
Trust Level Not critical Essential for success
Dispute Response Litigate and terminate Negotiate and preserve relationship
Investments Generic, reusable elsewhere Relationship-specific, lost if deal ends

Case Study: Boston Scientific & Medinol

The Parties and the Deal

Boston Scientific (BSC): Large US medical device company with global sales force

Medinol: Small Israeli company that invented innovative coronary stent technology

The Partnership:

  • Medinol develops and manufactures the stents
  • BSC markets and sells them worldwide through its distribution network
  • Long-term exclusive relationship
  • Both parties make substantial relationship-specific investments

Why This Is a Classic Relational Contract

  1. Long-term commitment: Multi-year exclusive relationship, not one-time transaction
  2. Mutual dependence:
    • Medinol needed BSC's global sales force and regulatory expertise
    • BSC needed Medinol's innovative technology to compete
  3. Relationship-specific investments:
    • Medinol built production capacity specifically for BSC's forecasted volumes
    • BSC trained its sales force on Medinol's specific technology
    • Both invested in joint regulatory approvals
    • These investments would lose most of their value if the relationship ended
  4. Uncertain future: Impossible to predict market conditions, competitive landscape, or technology developments years in advance
  5. Requires ongoing cooperation: Product improvements, quality issues, volume adjustments, market changes all require working together

Key Contract Provisions

1. Volume Forecasts

What the contract said: BSC would provide Medinol with periodic volume forecasts

Why this mattered: Medinol needed to know how much production capacity to build

The critical ambiguity: Were these forecasts binding commitments or merely non-binding estimates?

2. Alternative Products Clause

What the contract said: BSC retained the right to develop and sell competing stent products

Why BSC wanted this: Didn't want to be locked in if Medinol failed to deliver or if better technology emerged

Medinol's concern: BSC might prioritize its own products over Medinol's

3. Integration Clause

What it said: "This written contract is the entire agreement between the parties"

Legal effect: No side agreements, verbal promises, or informal understandings are enforceable

In court: Judge can only consider what's written in the contract, not "he promised me" claims

What Went Wrong

The Sequence of Events:

  1. BSC provided Medinol with volume forecasts showing substantial growth
  2. Relying on these forecasts, Medinol invested millions in expanding production capacity
  3. BSC's actual purchase orders came in far below the forecasted amounts
  4. Medinol was left with expensive excess capacity and mounting losses
  5. Medinol sued BSC for bad faith

BSC's Defense:

  • Forecasts are explicitly non-binding estimates, not purchase commitments
  • Contract clearly stated BSC had no obligation to buy forecasted amounts
  • Market conditions changed unexpectedly (economic downturn, regulatory issues)
  • BSC was contractually permitted to sell competing products
  • We acted within our contractual rights

Medinol's Counterargument:

  • BSC provided unrealistically high forecasts knowing Medinol would make major investments based on them
  • BSC deliberately shifted focus to its own competing products instead of promoting Medinol's
  • Even if technically within contract terms, BSC violated the spirit and good faith of the partnership
  • BSC exploited Medinol's dependence and relationship-specific investments

The Legal Tensions

1. Are Volume Forecasts Binding?

General Rule: Forecasts are predictions, not promises—they're typically non-binding

Exception: If given in bad faith (deliberately misleading to induce reliance), they might be actionable under promissory estoppel or fraud theories

The question: Did BSC give inflated forecasts in bad faith, or did market conditions genuinely change?

2. Implied Duty of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

The law: All contracts contain an implied obligation of good faith

What it means: Can't deliberately undermine the purpose of the contract or exploit the other party's vulnerability

Applied here: Did BSC violate good faith by:

  • Providing inflated forecasts to induce Medinol's investments?
  • Prioritizing its own competing products over Medinol's?
  • Failing to make reasonable efforts to meet forecasts?

3. Flexibility vs Certainty in Relational Contracts

The fundamental tension:

  • Relational contracts need flexibility because the future is unpredictable
  • BUT parties need some certainty to justify making relationship-specific investments
  • How much deviation from forecasts/expectations is acceptable before it becomes bad faith?

BSC/Medinol illustrates: When one party has made irreversible investments based on the other's representations, how much flexibility should the law allow?

Key Provisions in Commercial Contracts

1. Effort Standards

Contracts often require parties to use certain levels of effort. Different standards impose different obligations:

"Best Efforts":

  • Must do everything reasonably possible, even if costly
  • Highest standard—requires significant sacrifice
  • Example: "Seller shall use best efforts to obtain regulatory approval"

"Commercially Reasonable Efforts":

  • Must take reasonable steps consistent with normal business practices
  • Medium standard—balanced approach
  • Don't have to do things that would be economically irrational

"Reasonable Efforts":

  • Similar to commercially reasonable, but slightly less demanding
  • Must act reasonably but not heroically

2. Material Adverse Change (MAC) Clause

What It Is: Allows a party (usually acquirer) to walk away from a deal if target company experiences a material adverse change before closing.

Purpose: Protects buyer from unexpected bad events between signing and closing

Common Exclusions:

  • General economic conditions (recession affects everyone)
  • Industry-wide changes (regulatory changes affecting all competitors)
  • Changes resulting from the announcement of the deal itself
  • Natural disasters or pandemics (unless disproportionately affecting target)

Heavily negotiated: What counts as "material"? How severe must the change be?

3. Force Majeure

What It Is: Excuses performance when extraordinary events beyond the parties' control make performance impossible or impractical.

Typical Force Majeure Events:

  • Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, hurricanes)
  • Wars, terrorism, civil unrest
  • Pandemics
  • Government actions (embargoes, expropriation)
  • Strikes or labor disputes

Effect: Suspends obligations without penalty during the force majeure event

Does NOT: Automatically terminate the contract or excuse payment obligations already due

4. Warranties and Representations

Representations: Statements of fact that are true as of signing

Warranties: Promises that certain facts are and will remain true

Common Examples:

  • Product quality meets specified standards
  • No pending litigation against the company
  • No infringement of third-party intellectual property
  • Financial statements are accurate
  • Company has authority to enter into contract

Breach Consequences: If representations/warranties are false, injured party can sue for damages or terminate

5. Indemnification

What It Is: One party agrees to reimburse the other for losses from specified events

Example: "Seller shall indemnify Buyer for any losses arising from third-party IP infringement claims"

Key Issues:

  • What's covered? (breach of warranties, third-party claims, specific risks)
  • Any caps or limits?
  • Survival period—how long does indemnity last?
  • Procedures for making claims

Practice Questions

Question 1: Relational vs Ordinary Contracts

Explain the key differences between relational contracts and ordinary contracts. Provide one example of each type and explain why the characteristics of relational contracts make them both necessary and challenging.

Show Sample Answer

Key Differences:

Ordinary Contracts: One-time transactions where parties can specify all terms upfront. Example: buying a car. The price, vehicle specifications, and delivery date are all clear. If there's a breach, the injured party sues and walks away—there's no ongoing relationship to preserve.

Relational Contracts: Long-term relationships where the future is uncertain and complete specification is impossible. Example: a 10-year supplier agreement. The parties can't predict future market conditions, technology changes, or volume needs. They need flexibility to adapt.

Why Relational Contracts Are Necessary:

  • Some business relationships require long-term commitments and relationship-specific investments
  • Parties can't foresee all contingencies years in advance
  • Value comes from ongoing cooperation and adaptation, not one-time exchange

Why They're Challenging:

  • Flexibility creates ambiguity—when has someone breached vs just adapted?
  • Parties make irreversible investments based on expectations that may not be contractually enforceable
  • Trust is essential but hard to enforce legally
  • Disputes are costly because the relationship itself has value that litigation destroys

Question 2: BSC/Medinol Case Analysis

In the BSC/Medinol case, explain what went wrong in the relationship. What legal arguments did each side make, and what does this case illustrate about the tension between flexibility and certainty in relational contracts?

Show Sample Answer

What Went Wrong:

  • BSC gave Medinol volume forecasts showing substantial growth
  • Medinol invested millions in production capacity based on these forecasts
  • BSC's actual orders fell far short of forecasts
  • Medinol was left with expensive excess capacity and mounting losses

BSC's Legal Argument:

  • Forecasts were explicitly non-binding estimates, not purchase commitments
  • Contract clearly stated no obligation to buy forecasted amounts
  • Market conditions changed unexpectedly
  • BSC stayed within its contractual rights to sell competing products

Medinol's Legal Argument:

  • BSC gave unrealistic forecasts in bad faith to induce Medinol's investments
  • BSC violated the implied duty of good faith by prioritizing competing products
  • Even if technically legal, BSC exploited Medinol's relationship-specific investments

The Tension Illustrated:

Relational contracts need flexibility because the future is unpredictable—BSC couldn't commit to exact volumes years in advance. BUT Medinol needed some certainty to justify massive investments. The case shows the difficulty of balancing these competing needs: too much flexibility leaves one party vulnerable to exploitation; too much rigidity makes adaptation impossible. The law must navigate between enforcing literal contract terms (which protects flexibility) and the duty of good faith (which protects reasonable reliance).
