Class 5: Term Sheets

Understanding venture capital deal terms and who wants what

🎧

Class 5 Podcast

Listen first for overview (~12 minutes)

🎯 EXAM FOCUS: For EVERY term sheet provision, know WHO wants it and WHY they want it. This is the key to answering term sheet questions correctly.

Economic Terms

1. Liquidation Preference

What It Is: VCs get paid first (and potentially extra) when the company exits through sale or liquidation.

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Downside protection—if the exit is disappointing, VCs at least get their money back before common stockholders get anything.

The Mechanics:

  • 1X preference: Get investment back first, then split remaining proceeds pro-rata with common
  • 2X or 3X preference: Get 2X or 3X investment back first (stronger protection for VCs)

Non-Participating vs Participating:

Non-Participating: VC chooses EITHER preference OR convert to common (whichever is higher)

  • Low exit → take preference
  • High exit → convert to common

Participating: VC gets BOTH preference AND pro-rata share (double-dipping)

  • Much better for VCs, much worse for founders
  • Sometimes called "participating preferred with cap"

Founders Prefer: 1X non-participating (least favorable to VCs)

VCs Prefer: Multiple X participating (most favorable to VCs)

2. Dividends

What It Is: Regular payments to preferred stockholders, similar to bond interest.

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Get returns even if no exit happens soon; provides ongoing cash flow.

Types:

  • Cumulative: If not paid, dividends accumulate as debt to VCs (pile up over time)
  • Non-cumulative: If not paid in a given year, they're forfeited
  • Typical rate: 6-8% annually

Founders Prefer: No dividends at all—startups need cash for growth, not distributions

Reality: Most VC deals have no mandatory dividends because early-stage companies are cash-constrained

3. Anti-Dilution Protection

What It Is: If company raises money at a lower price per share (down round), VCs get additional shares to maintain their investment value.

WHO Wants It: VCs (especially early investors)

WHY They Want It: Protection if company's valuation drops; prevents being "punished" for investing early at a higher price.

Types:

  • Full ratchet: VC's price adjusts completely to the new lower price (very harsh on founders)
  • Weighted average: Blends old and new price based on investment amounts (more fair to both sides)

WHO Bears the Cost: Common stockholders (founders) get diluted extra to give VCs more shares

When It Matters: Only triggers in down rounds (when new round price < previous round price)

4. Valuation Cap (Convertible Notes)

What It Is: Maximum valuation at which a convertible note converts to equity.

WHO Wants It: Early investors (using convertible notes instead of priced equity rounds)

WHY They Want It: Limits their downside if company's valuation skyrockets before conversion; ensures they get rewarded for early risk.

Example:

  • Investor puts $100K into convertible note with $5M cap
  • Company later raises Series A at $20M valuation
  • Note converts at the $5M cap (not $20M)
  • Investor gets 4X more shares than Series A investors per dollar invested

Control and Governance Terms

5. Board Composition

What It Is: Who sits on the board of directors and makes major strategic decisions.

Common Structure:

  • 2 seats for founders/management
  • 2 seats for VCs/investors
  • 1 independent seat (mutually agreed upon)

WHO Wants Board Seats: Both VCs and founders

WHY VCs Want Them:

  • Oversight and monitoring of their investment
  • Ability to influence major decisions
  • Protect their investment from founder mistakes
  • Remove CEO if necessary

WHY Founders Want Them:

  • Maintain operational control
  • Drive strategic direction
  • Avoid being fired from their own company

6. Protective Provisions

What It Is: List of major actions that require VC approval (essentially veto power over certain decisions).

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Prevent founders from taking actions that could harm VC interests.

Typical Protected Actions:

  • Issuing new stock: Prevents dilution of VCs without their consent
  • Selling the company: VCs want control over exit timing and terms
  • Changing stock rights: Can't reduce VC privileges unilaterally
  • Incurring debt above threshold: Limits financial risk
  • Changing business model: Can't pivot away from what VCs invested in
  • Declaring dividends: Prevents founders from paying themselves instead of reinvesting

7. Drag-Along Rights

What It Is: If a majority of shareholders vote to sell the company, minority shareholders are forced to sell too on the same terms.

WHO Wants It: VCs and majority shareholders

WHY They Want It: Prevent one stubborn minority shareholder from blocking a good exit opportunity.

Example Scenario:

  • Acquirer offers to buy 100% of company for good price
  • VCs + founders agree (representing 90% of shares)
  • One early employee with 2% refuses to sell
  • Acquirer wants 100% ownership, not 98%
  • Drag-along forces the 2% minority to sell
  • Deal closes successfully

Without Drag-Along: The entire acquisition could fall apart over one small minority holder

8. Tag-Along Rights (Co-Sale Rights)

What It Is: If founders sell their shares to a third party, VCs can sell their shares too on the same terms.

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Prevent founders from cashing out and abandoning ship while VCs remain locked in.

Example Scenario:

  • Founder receives offer from private buyer to purchase 20% of founder's stake
  • VCs can "tag along" and sell their shares to the same buyer on identical terms
  • Ensures everyone has equal liquidity opportunities
  • Prevents founders from selling early while VCs are stuck

9. Right of First Refusal (ROFR)

What It Is: If a shareholder wants to sell shares, the company (or existing investors) gets the first opportunity to buy them.

WHO Wants It: Company and existing investors

WHY They Want It: Control who becomes a shareholder; keep ownership within the existing group.

How It Works:

  1. Employee receives outside offer to buy their 10,000 shares at $5/share
  2. Company has 30 days to match the $5/share offer and buy the shares
  3. If company declines, VCs have 30 days to match the offer
  4. Only if both company and VCs decline can employee sell to the outside buyer

Other Important Terms

10. Vesting

What It Is: Founders earn their shares over time (typically 4 years) rather than owning them all immediately.

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Ensure founders stay committed to the company; prevent founders from leaving early with all their equity.

Standard Vesting Schedule:

  • 4-year total vesting period
  • 1-year cliff: Nothing vests for the first year, then 25% vests all at once
  • Monthly vesting after cliff: Remaining 75% vests in equal monthly increments over 36 months

Example:

Founder receives 1,000,000 shares with standard 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff:

  • Month 11 (before cliff): Quits → gets 0 shares
  • Month 13 (after cliff): Quits → gets 250,000 shares (25%)
  • Month 25: Quits → gets 500,000 shares (cliff + 12 more months)
  • Year 4 completion: Gets all 1,000,000 shares

11. Information Rights

What It Is: VCs receive regular financial statements, reports, and updates about company performance.

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Monitor their investment; no surprises about company performance.

Typical Information Rights:

  • Monthly unaudited financial statements
  • Annual audited financial statements
  • Annual budget and business plan
  • Quarterly board meeting attendance rights
  • Access to company facilities for inspection

12. Registration Rights

What It Is: VCs can force the company to file for an IPO or include VC shares when company goes public.

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Ensure they can exit their investment via public markets; prevents being locked into illiquid stock forever.

Types:

  • Demand rights: VCs can force the company to register for IPO (usually after certain timeframe or conditions)
  • Piggyback rights: If company decides to do an IPO, VCs can include their shares in the offering

13. No-Shop Clause

What It Is: Founders cannot negotiate with other VCs for a specified period (typically 30-60 days).

WHO Wants It: VCs

WHY They Want It: Prevent being used as leverage while conducting due diligence; ensures exclusive negotiation period.

Typical Duration: 30-60 days

Founders' Concern: Gets locked into one VC without ability to create competitive pressure

14. Pay-to-Play

What It Is: Existing investors lose certain privileges if they don't invest their pro-rata share in future funding rounds.

WHO Wants It: Lead investors in new rounds (especially down rounds)

WHY They Want It: Punish investors who won't support a struggling company when it needs more capital.

Example:

  • Company raises Series C at lower valuation (down round)
  • Series C investors add pay-to-play provision
  • Series A and B investors must invest their pro-rata share
  • If they don't: their preferred stock converts to common, they lose board seat, lose liquidation preference

✓ How to Answer Term Sheet Questions on the Exam

Structure Every Answer This Way:

  1. What it is: One-sentence definition of the term
  2. Who wants it: VCs, Founders, or Both
  3. Why they want it: The economic or control motivation
  4. Example: Concrete scenario showing how it works

Sample Answer for Drag-Along Rights:

"Drag-along rights allow majority shareholders to force minority shareholders to sell their shares in an acquisition. VCs want this provision because it prevents a small minority shareholder from blocking a good exit opportunity for everyone. For example, if 90% of shareholders (VCs + founders) agree to sell the company to an acquirer who wants 100% ownership, drag-along forces the remaining 10% to sell on the same terms, ensuring the deal can close."
